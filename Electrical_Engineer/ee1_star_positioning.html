The Core Distinction to Land in Every Answer

An EET uses instruments. An EE I builds the systems those instruments run on — models failure mathematically before touching the board, automates the measurement sequence with SCPI, and produces engineering documentation with quantified findings. Every conversation below drives that distinction home using work you actually built.

Electronic Engineering Technician

  • Measures rail voltages at test points
  • Reads waveforms on an oscilloscope
  • Identifies that a component is bad
  • Follows a documented test procedure
  • Replaces the component and re-tests
  • Logs pass/fail in MES

Electrical Engineer I — Your Position

  • Calculates expected rail behavior before probing
  • Writes SCPI automation that runs the test sequence
  • Derives root cause from transfer function math
  • Designs the test procedure from engineering analysis
  • Selects the replacement component analytically
  • Generates an engineering report with quantified findings

"Tell me about a time you diagnosed a hardware problem using engineering analysis rather than just trial and error."
Hiring Manager

"So walk me through what you've been doing to prepare for this role."

You Situation

"Sure. I built out a full engineering study workspace — not just reading notes, but actually building tools. I have three connected folders: FE exam theory, electrical engineering design, and a technician-level folder with real PCB troubleshooting scenarios. The thing I want to walk you through is a power rail failure scenario I built, because it shows exactly how I approach problems."

"The board had intermittent resets. The 3.3 volt rail was sagging below the MCU's brownout threshold. A technician looks at that and swaps the bypass capacitor. What I did instead was model it first."

You Task

"My goal was to derive the root cause from the math before touching anything — calculate what the scope should show based on the component parameters, then confirm it with actual measurements. That loop — model first, measure second — is the difference between a technician replacing parts until something works and an engineer closing the loop between the physics and the data."

You Action

"I started with the RC transient formula — tau equals ESR times C. A healthy 22 microfarad bypass cap with 0.05 ohm ESR gives you a 1 microsecond time constant, meaning the rail recovers almost instantly after a load step. But if that ESR degrades to 2 ohms, tau jumps to 44 microseconds, and the instantaneous voltage drop is I times ESR — at 150 milliamps that's 300 millivolts. That crosses the 2.97 volt brownout threshold. So I knew the root cause from the math before I even picked up a probe."

"Then I wrote a Python script using scipy and matplotlib that plotted both cases side by side so I could visualize the sag depth and recovery time. And then I wrote a pyvisa automation script that sent real SCPI commands to a bench power supply, a DMM, and an oscilloscope — measured the ESR at 2.0 ohms, the rail minimum at 2.88 volts, the ripple at 1 kilohertz — exactly what the model predicted."

Hiring Manager

"What did the documentation look like at the end of that?"

You Result

"The NCR came out with engineering justification — not just 'replaced C2 and it worked,' but a calculated failure mode with a quantified ESR threshold, a time constant comparison between the good and bad cap, and scope data showing the rail sag crossing the brownout line. That's something an engineer produces, not a technician. And because it's a model, not a one-time fix — if that same failure shows up on a different board with a different cap value, I recalculate tau for the new C and the answer scales."

EE I Lift

You're not just fixing the symptom. You're building a failure model that applies across the product line. That's the engineering judgment that makes you worth more than someone replacing parts until the board passes.

"How do you apply signal processing knowledge to actual hardware work?"
You Situation

"There's a problem that comes up constantly on power rails — you can see on the oscilloscope that the signal is noisy, but the time-domain waveform doesn't tell you where the noise is coming from. Switching regulator, a clock harmonic, external coupling — they all look like ripple on the screen. Without frequency-domain analysis you're guessing at the source."

You Task

"I wanted a workflow that moves from a time-domain oscilloscope capture to a frequency-domain signature automatically — so the noise source is identified by the data, not by instinct."

You Action

"I wrote a Python pipeline that uses the scope's SCPI interface — specifically :WAVeform:DATA? and :WAVeform:PREamble? — to pull raw ADC samples directly into numpy. From there np.fft.rfft() converts the capture to the frequency domain. I plot both views side by side: time domain on top, FFT spectrum on the bottom, annotated peaks."

"When I ran this on the noisy 3.3 volt rail, the FFT immediately showed three peaks — 1 kilohertz fundamental from the switching regulator, a 3 kilohertz third harmonic from nonlinear switching behavior, and a 50 kilohertz transient artifact. None of those were distinct in the time-domain view. And in terms of theory, each peak corresponds to a pair of imaginary poles in the system — the same concept as a Laplace sinusoidal response from FE exam Linear Systems, just applied to a real power rail measurement."

Hiring Manager

"How does knowing the frequency actually change what you do on the bench?"

You Result

"It completely changes where you look. A 1 kilohertz peak points at the switching regulator — check the output filter or layout. A harmonic series points at nonlinear behavior — check the switcher's feedback loop or output impedance. A broadband noise floor points at EMI pickup — check shielding and grounding. Once you know the frequency you're not guessing anymore, you're following the data to the source. And that output belongs in the engineering report alongside the scope screenshot — it's evidence, not opinion."

EE I Lift

You're connecting the FE exam Linear Systems topic — Laplace transforms, imaginary poles, frequency response — to a real diagnostic workflow. The technician reads the scope. The engineer models the system and explains what the scope is showing.

"Give me an example of a project where you had to work across both hardware and software."
You Situation

"I was looking at ringing on a digital clock edge — the underdamped oscillation you see when a driver is fighting trace inductance and load capacitance. It's a common signal integrity issue, and the question is always: is this benign, or is the overshoot large enough to cause logic errors or stress the component?"

You Task

"I needed to go from the oscilloscope measurement — specifically the overshoot percentage — to a quantified damping ratio, and from that to a specific component recommendation. Not just 'add a series termination resistor,' but tell me exactly what value gets me to critical damping."

You Action

"I used scipy's TransferFunction and step response to model the second-order system — H of s equals omega-n squared over s squared plus 2 zeta omega-n s plus omega-n squared. I ran it across three damping ratios: heavy ringing at 0.15, moderate at 0.495 which is the exact system from FE exam Q4, and critically damped at 1.0. The overshoot formula lets you work backwards — you measure the overshoot on the scope, solve for zeta, then calculate what resistance you need to bring zeta to 1.0."

"I also plotted the pole-zero map for each case, all poles in the left half-plane, confirming stability. That directly connects what you see on the scope — the ringing and the decay rate — to the mathematical structure: the imaginary part of the pole is the ringing frequency, the real part is how fast it dies out."

Hiring Manager

"So you can get from a scope measurement to a BOM change?"

You Result

"Exactly. When I see 17 percent overshoot on a clock edge I can calculate that the damping ratio is about 0.5. To get to critical damping I need to roughly double the real part of the poles — which means adding a series resistor equal to the characteristic impedance of the trace. That's the math telling me what goes on the BOM. And the Python model is reusable — change omega-n and zeta, rerun, get a new answer for any second-order system. It also creates an audit trail: you attach the model output to the design review and show exactly why that resistor value was chosen."

EE I Lift

This is the hardware-software loop that defines EE I work: measure → model → quantify → specify. You're not just fixing the board in front of you — you're building the analytical tool that handles every board with that failure mode.

"How do you approach component selection, and how do you validate the choice?"
You Situation

"Decoupling cap selection is something most engineers do by rule of thumb — put a 100 nanofarad and a 10 microfarad near every power pin. That rule works most of the time. But when it doesn't work, you need to know why — and if you don't understand the physics behind it, you can't adapt it when the constraints change."

You Task

"I wanted to be able to justify the cap selection analytically — explain why those specific values, and what changes if the trace geometry or switching frequency is different."

You Action

"I built an RLC Bode plot in Python that models the decoupling network as a series RLC — trace inductance from the PCB layout, cap value from the BOM, ESR from the datasheet. The impedance minimum occurs at omega-naught equals one over root LC. That's the frequency where the cap most effectively absorbs switching energy. I swept four cap values from 100 nanofarads to 100 microfarads and plotted impedance magnitude and phase from 1 kilohertz to 1 gigahertz on a log scale."

"The result makes the design decision visual: a 100 nanofarad cap on a 10 millimeter trace has its minimum at 5 megahertz — great for a 5 megahertz switcher, useless at 300 kilohertz. A 10 microfarad cap moves that minimum down to 500 kilohertz. To cover both frequencies you use both in parallel — and now I know the reason for the rule, so I can adapt it when the switching frequency changes or the trace has to be longer. For validation I run the scope in FFT mode after assembly and confirm the attenuation at the switching frequency matches the model. If it doesn't, I check the trace length in Altium's PCB Inspector — a longer trace means more inductance, which shifts the resonance lower than predicted."

Hiring Manager

"How does that tie into the CAD side of the role?"

You Result

"They feed into each other directly. IPC-2221 gives me the trace width for current capacity on that net. My inductance model gives me the trace length limit for noise performance. Together they define the complete design constraint — you can't just make the trace shorter for better noise without checking that it's still wide enough to carry the current. That's a tradeoff an EE I owns. And after any rework I run DRC in Altium to confirm no clearance violations were introduced, and if the board has JTAG I run boundary-scan to confirm net connectivity before powering back up. The Python analysis and the CAD verification aren't separate workflows — they're the same engineering decision seen from two different angles."

EE I Lift

You understand the physics behind the design rule, not just the rule itself. That's what lets you handle edge cases — non-standard layouts, unusual switching frequencies, cost-constrained BOM changes — without escalating to a senior engineer every time something deviates from the standard.

CAD Notes

Altium Designer · OrCAD · Eagle · IPC-2221

Altium — Drop These Terms

  • Reference PCB Inspector when discussing trace length and impedance — shows you know the tool, not just the concept
  • Mention DRC as the automated check after every layout change
  • Net Inspector for connectivity verification after rework
  • Layer Stack Manager → impedance calculation is an EE I task, not EET

IPC-2221 — Tie to Q5

  • Formula: Area(mil²) = [I / (k × ΔT0.44)]1/0.725
  • k = 0.048 external layer, k = 0.024 internal layer
  • Pair with trace inductance model: length drives both current capacity (width) and noise performance (L)
  • This pairing is the complete design constraint for a power net — mention it in Q5

OrCAD / PSpice — Position Your Python Work

  • scipy models mirror what PSpice does — frame your Python work as a scripting-layer equivalent
  • OrCAD schematic → BOM extraction → Python ESR/Bode model is a complete analytical workflow
  • If they use OrCAD, mention PSpice by name — shows cross-tool awareness

SCPI in ATE Context

  • ATE systems — LabVIEW, Python fixtures — all use SCPI under the hood; pyvisa is the Python binding
  • Position your knowledge as: "I understand the command layer, not just the GUI"
  • Name specific commands: *IDN?, :WAVeform:DATA?, MEASure:VOLTage:DC? — specifics show depth
  • IVI-COM / VISA driver architecture is the standard — pyvisa implements it

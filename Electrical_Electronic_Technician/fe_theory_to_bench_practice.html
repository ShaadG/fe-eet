How Q1 Appears on the Bench

The FE exam gives you a clean step input and asks you to calculate vC at a specific time. On the bench, you're doing the inverse: you see the waveform on the oscilloscope and need to infer τ from the shape.

vC(t) = V(1 − e−t/τ)  →  τ = RC
On bench: R = ESR of bypass cap, C = capacitance · High ESR → large τ → slow recovery → brownout
τ = 2.0Ω × 22µF = 44µs  (degraded C2)  vs.  τ = 0.05Ω × 22µF = 1.1µs (good MLCC)
1
Recognizing τ on the scope: Zoom to the recovery edge after a sag. The voltage rises from the sag floor to 63.2% of its total recovery in exactly one τ. Measure that time division → τ.
2
Calculating ESR from τ: τ = ESR × C. Rearrange: ESR = τ / C. If you measure τ = 44µs and C = 22µF → ESR = 2.0Ω. Compare to the component's datasheet maximum ESR (typically < 0.05Ω for a good MLCC at this value).
3
Why V = 10V in the FE plot vs. 3.3V on the bench: The math is identical — only the supply voltage and τ values change. The exponential shape is universal. This is why FE exam math transfers directly to bench work.
"I studied the RC transient formula for the FE exam — vC(t) = V(1−e−t/τ). When I built the power rail simulation in my study folder, I realized that's the exact same equation that describes a bypass cap charging during an MCU load transient. The time constant τ = ESR × C tells you how fast the voltage recovers. A degraded cap with ESR of 2Ω has τ = 44µs — long enough that the voltage sags past the MCU's 2.97V brownout threshold. That's where the FE exam math directly explains a real failure mode."

How Q2 Appears on the Bench

The FE exam asks you to find ω₀ and Q for an RLC circuit. On a PCB, every power rail trace has parasitic inductance (~1nH/mm) and every decoupling capacitor adds capacitance. Together they form an LC filter — or, if not designed carefully, an LC resonator that amplifies noise at ω₀.

ω₀ = 1/√(LC)  →  f₀ = ω₀/(2π)
Trace: 10mm trace ≈ 10nH parasitic inductance. Cap: 100nF bypass cap
f₀ = 1/(2π × √(10×10⁻⁹ × 100×10⁻⁹)) = 1/(2π × 1µs) ≈ 159 kHz
A switching regulator at 159kHz would drive maximum noise into this power rail
1
Measuring noise frequency on the scope: AC couple the probe on the power rail. Measure the dominant frequency of the ripple using time/div. If ripple period = 6.3µs → f = 159kHz → compare to regulator switching frequency and LC resonant frequency.
2
Decoupling cap selection: To move ω₀ away from the regulator frequency, change C. Lower C → higher ω₀; higher C → lower ω₀. The FE exam Q formula (Q = ω₀L/R) tells you how sharp the resonance is — high Q = narrow band but severe amplification at exactly ω₀.
3
Multiple caps in parallel: A 100nF + 10µF combination provides low impedance across a wider frequency range — the 100nF handles high-frequency noise (MHz), the 10µF handles lower-frequency load transients (kHz).
"The RLC resonance question from the FE exam is directly relevant to why PCBs use multiple decoupling capacitor values in parallel. ω₀ = 1/√(LC) tells you the frequency at which a particular cap-trace combination has minimum impedance — and therefore maximum noise suppression. If the switching regulator runs near that frequency, the decoupling works well. If it doesn't, you choose a different cap value to tune ω₀ to where you need it."

How Q3 Appears on the Bench

The FE exam asks you to find the Laplace transform of a sinusoidal function. On the bench, the oscilloscope displays AC ripple that looks exactly like 3sin(2t) — just scaled to different amplitude and frequency. The pole map from Q3 explains a subtle but important point: why does the ripple sustain without dying out?

F(s) = ω/(s² + ω²)  →  poles at s = ±jω
Purely imaginary poles → zero real part → e0·t = 1 → no exponential decay
A sustained sine wave = poles exactly on the jω axis (marginally stable)
If poles were in left half-plane: signal would decay. Right half-plane: signal would grow.
1
Reading frequency from the scope: The scope displays exactly f(t) = A·sin(2πf·t). Count divisions per cycle in time: if one cycle spans 5 horizontal divisions at 0.2ms/div → T = 5 × 0.2ms = 1ms → f = 1kHz → ω = 2π×1000 = 6283 rad/s.
2
Pole location tells you signal character: The FE pole map for Q3 shows poles at exactly ±j2. For a 1kHz ripple waveform, the poles are at ±j6283 — the same structure, different scale. Purely imaginary = sustained sine. No real component = no decay, no growth.
3
AC coupling reveals only the sinusoidal component: DC coupling shows the full rail (3.3V DC + ripple). AC coupling blocks the DC component, showing only the oscillatory part — exactly the f(t) = A·sin(ωt) that maps to the Laplace transform in Q3.
"The Q3 Laplace transform has poles at ±j2 — purely imaginary, zero real part. That tells you the signal is a sustained sinusoid that neither grows nor decays. When I see AC ripple on a power rail that maintains a consistent amplitude cycle after cycle, those are the same pole conditions. The Laplace framework tells you what kind of signal you're looking at just from the pole locations — which is exactly what an oscilloscope's AC coupling mode is isolating."

How Q4 Appears on the Bench

Every time a CMOS output switches from LOW to HIGH, it drives through a PCB trace that has parasitic inductance. The receiving IC has input capacitance. Together: a second-order system. If ζ < 1, the step response rings — exactly the H(s) = ωn²/(s² + 2ζωns + ωn²) transfer function from Q4.

Poles of H(s) = 50/(s²+7s+50):
s = (−7 ± √(49−200))/2 = −3.5 ± j6.14
Real part −3.5 → exponential decay envelope e−3.5t
Imaginary part j6.14 → oscillation at ωd = 6.14 rad/s (scaled to circuit frequency in practice)
ζ = 7/(2√50) = 0.495 → underdamped (0 < ζ < 1) → ringing occurs
1
Identifying ringing on the scope: Zoom to 10–50 ns/div on a clock edge. If the waveform overshoots and oscillates before settling, the system is underdamped (ζ < 1). Count the number of oscillation cycles before the waveform settles to estimate ζ: more cycles = lower ζ.
2
Stability check from pole locations: Q4's poles are at −3.5 ± j6.14 — in the left half-plane (real part negative). The system is stable: ringing decays to zero. If poles were in the right half-plane (positive real part), the ringing would grow → oscillator or instability. Poles exactly on the jω axis → sustained ringing forever.
3
Reducing ringing on a PCB: Add a series termination resistor (33Ω) at the output pin. This increases the real part of the system's effective damping, moving ζ toward 1 (critically damped) and eliminating overshoot. IPC-2221 trace length and impedance matching also affect the ringing amplitude.
Scope measurement tip: Overshoot % = (V_peak − V_final) / V_final × 100%. For Q4's ζ = 0.495: theoretical overshoot ≈ e−πζ/√(1−ζ²) × 100% ≈ 16.5%. On the scope, measure the first peak above the settled voltage and compare to the settled level. This gives you an experimental estimate of ζ without knowing L or C.
"The FE exam Q4 transfer function has poles at −3.5 ± j6.14. That means ζ = 0.495 — underdamped. When I see ringing on a digital clock edge on the oscilloscope, I know I'm looking at a second-order underdamped system: trace inductance + load capacitance forming H(s). The ringing decays because the poles are in the left half-plane — the system is stable. If I needed to fix it, I'd add a series termination resistor to increase damping."
New Waveform — EET Bench Reference Triangle Wave — PWM Carrier & RC Integrator Output Piecewise Linear · Harmonic Series 1/n² · Slew Rate
Theory — What Makes a Triangle Wave

A triangle wave is a piecewise-linear periodic signal. Unlike a square wave (infinite bandwidth) or sine wave (single frequency), it occupies a middle ground: only odd harmonics, falling off as 1/n² — far less high-frequency energy than a square wave.

v(t) = (8A/π²) Σ [( −1)ⁿ / (2n+1)²] · sin((2n+1)ωt)
n = 0, 1, 2, ...   ·   Only odd harmonics (same as square wave)
Amplitudes ∝ 1/n² — 3rd harmonic is 1/9 of fundamental (vs. 1/3 for square)
Result: much lower EMI than a square wave at the same frequency

Key measurements on the scope:

  • Amplitude (peak-to-peak)
  • Frequency (from period T)
  • Rise time = fall time = T/2 (symmetric triangle)
  • Slew rate = 2A/T (V/s) — the slope of the linear ramp
  • Symmetry check: if rise ≠ fall → circuit fault or asymmetric drive

Where You See Triangle Waves on a PCB

Application 1 — PWM Controller Carrier Signal: Inside a switching voltage regulator or PWM motor driver IC, a triangle (or sawtooth) wave is generated internally and compared against a reference voltage. The comparator output goes HIGH when the triangle exceeds the reference, LOW when it falls below. The duty cycle of the PWM output = (reference voltage / peak triangle voltage) × 100%. If the PWM frequency shifts unexpectedly, the internal oscillator RC circuit may have drifted — and you can observe the triangle wave on a test pin with the scope.
Application 2 — RC Integrator Output: If you drive an RC low-pass filter with a square wave where RC >> T/2, the capacitor never fully charges or discharges — it sees only the linear portion of the exponential. The output approximates a triangle wave. This is a classic bench verification test: apply a known square wave, observe the triangle output, confirm the RC time constant from the slope.

Slew rate verification: Slope = ΔV/Δt = (V_supply × duty_cycle) / (R × C). Compare measured slope to calculated — a discrepancy points to incorrect R or C values, or a capacitor with high ESR (which reduces effective capacitance).
Slew rate = 2 × V_peak / T = 2 × V_peak × f
Example: V_peak = 2.5V, f = 1kHz → Slew rate = 2 × 2.5 × 1000 = 5000 V/s = 5 mV/µs
Measure on scope: ΔV between two cursors / Δt between same cursors = slew rate

"A triangle wave shows up in two places on a PCB: as the carrier signal inside a PWM switching regulator, and as the output of an RC integrator driven by a square wave. The Fourier series for a triangle wave has only odd harmonics, but they fall off as 1/n² instead of 1/n for a square wave — so a triangle wave generates far less EMI. On the scope, I'd verify a triangle wave by measuring the slew rate (slope = 2A/T) and checking that rise time equals fall time — asymmetry indicates a circuit problem."

Footprint Verification — Decoupling Caps (C2 / RC & RLC Context)

Every decoupling capacitor involved in the RC transient and RLC resonance scenarios (Q1, Q2) has a physical footprint on the PCB. Before replacing C2 or selecting a new cap value, verify the following in the Altium board file:

1
Altium → Tools → PCB Inspector → select C2: Confirm footprint is 0805 (2012 metric). If the replacement cap is 0402, the footprint is incompatible — a rework technician cannot place it without a board-level ECO.
2
Component clearance check (DRC): After a footprint change, run Design → Design Rule Check → Clearance. Confirm the replacement pad does not violate minimum clearance to adjacent components. A 0805 cap has larger pads than 0402 — direction matters.
3
Net Inspector → 3V3 rail net: Confirm C2 is connected to both the 3V3 power net and GND net. If the net connection is missing (dangling pad), DRC will flag it — but a tech replacing the component without checking the schematic can accidentally solder it to the wrong pads.
4
OrCAD / PSpice connection: The Q1 RC transient plot and Q2 RLC resonance plot can both be validated using OrCAD PSpice before any physical change. Simulate the new cap value's ESR in the model and confirm the voltage at TP3 stays above 2.97V during a 150mA load step.

Trace Width — IPC-2221 for Power Rail Carrying RC/RLC Current

The 3.3V rail that feeds into the RC and RLC circuits on PCBA-REV2 carries up to 500mA. IPC-2221 defines the minimum trace width required for that current without exceeding the allowable temperature rise.

IPC-2221: Area (mil²) = [I / (k × ΔT0.44)]1/0.725
k = 0.048 (external layer) · k = 0.024 (internal layer) · ΔT = 10°C max rise
I = 500mA: Area = [0.5 / (0.048 × 100.44)]1/0.725 = [0.5 / 0.132]1.379 ≈ 11.7 mil² → ~0.25mm width (1oz Cu, external)
Altium DRC trace width rule: In the Altium PCB editor, add a Width Constraint rule for the net 3V3: minimum = 0.25mm, preferred = 0.5mm. Run DRC to verify no existing trace on that net falls below the minimum. If a trace is undersized, the board runs hotter and the actual RC behavior deviates from the simulated model — the R in τ = RC is not just ESR; it includes trace resistance.

Net Impedance Review — Signal Integrity for Q4 (Ringing / Underdamped)

The ringing behavior described in Q4 is driven by trace inductance + capacitive load forming a second-order system. In Altium, the net impedance can be estimated using the Layer Stack Manager and Net Inspector:

1
Altium → Design → Layer Stack Manager: Identify the dielectric thickness between the signal layer and its reference (ground) plane. This determines characteristic impedance Z₀ for microstrip or stripline traces.
2
Microstrip impedance (approximate): Z₀ ≈ (87/√(εr + 1.41)) × ln(5.98h / (0.8w + t)). For FR4 (εr ≈ 4.5), h = 0.1mm, w = 0.15mm, t = 0.035mm → Z₀ ≈ 65Ω. A series termination resistor (33Ω at source) brings effective source impedance to ~65Ω, damping the ringing seen in Q4.
3
Net Inspector → CLOCK net → length: Trace length converts to parasitic inductance (~1nH/mm). A 30mm clock trace = ~30nH. With 10pF load capacitance: ω₀ = 1/√(30nH × 10pF) ≈ 58 Mrad/s → f₀ ≈ 9.2MHz. This sets the ringing frequency you observe on the scope — directly computable from the Q2 formula ω₀ = 1/√(LC).
4
Eagle alternative: In Eagle, use the RATSNEST and DRC commands equivalently. Signal integrity simulation requires an external tool (HyperLynx or SI capabilities in OrCAD/Sigrity), but trace length and width verification is available natively.
IPC-2221 reference for this file: Use the IPC-2221 trace width calculator at pcb_calculators.html to verify all power rail traces covered in Q1 and Q2 scenarios. The calculator uses k=0.048 (external) and k=0.024 (internal) per IPC-2221 Table 6-1.

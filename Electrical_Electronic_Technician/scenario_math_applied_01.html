1 — ICT False Reject vs. Real Defect

Probability · Normal Distribution · Guard Band Decision

The Floor Problem

A board fails ICT. R15 on the power management circuit reads 9,893 Ω. The spec is 10 kΩ ± 1%, so the lower spec limit (LSL) is 9,900 Ω — the reading is 7 Ω below the line. The board gets flagged for rework.

Before the tech touches it: is this a real defect, or did measurement noise cause the fail? The ICT tester's calibration record shows a 1σ uncertainty of 18 Ω at this range. That 7 Ω margin is less than half a sigma — well within the tester's noise floor.

Management pain point: Unnecessary rework adds 15–20 minutes of tech time per board, introduces handling risk (lifted pads, ESD), and pulls the board back through ICT for another cycle. At 50 boards per shift, bad rework decisions are a throughput and cost problem — not just a quality problem.

The Math — Normal Distribution Answers the Question

If R15's true value is exactly at the LSL (9,900 Ω) and the tester has σ = 18 Ω, what is the probability the tester reads ≤ 9,893 Ω just from measurement noise?

z = (x − µ) / σ = (9,893 − 9,900) / 18 = −0.39

P(reading ≤ 9,893 | true value = 9,900 Ω) = P(z ≤ −0.39)
≈ 34.8% — more than 1 in 3 chance this is a false reject

68.3% of readings fall within µ ± 1σ (±18 Ω)
95.4% of readings fall within µ ± 2σ (±36 Ω)
99.7% of readings fall within µ ± 3σ (±54 Ω) ← three-sigma rule

The Decision Rule — What the Tech Does on the Floor

A 34.8% false reject probability is too high to rework without verification. The correct action: take 5 more readings and compute the sample mean.

  • If the sample mean µ̄ < 9,900 Ω with consistent readings → real defect, rework and escalate
  • If the sample mean µ̄ ≥ 9,900 Ω → false reject from measurement noise, re-run ICT, skip rework
  • If readings are scattered (high σ) → suspect fixture probe wear, check contact resistance at that pin

This is also the logic behind guard banding: set the ICT test limit to LSL + 2σ = 9,900 + 36 = 9,936 Ω instead of 9,900 Ω. Any board reading below 9,936 Ω gets flagged for verification — not automatic rework. Boards with true values inside spec but near the edge get a second look; genuine defects still get caught.

Key Talking Point
"When a board fails ICT by a few ohms, the first question isn't 'where's the solder iron?' — it's 'is this reading real?' The tester has measurement uncertainty. If the margin between the reading and the spec limit is less than one sigma, statistically you're looking at a coin flip between a real defect and measurement noise. Management cares about both sides: unnecessary rework costs labor and introduces handling damage, and a false accept costs a field failure. The normal distribution gives you a number — a probability — so you make that call with data, not gut feel."

2 — Process Capability (Cpk)

Statistical Inference · Manufacturing Quality

The Concept

Cpk measures how well a manufacturing process keeps its output within spec limits. It combines the mean (centering) and standard deviation (spread) of a population into a single quality index. Industry standard target is Cpk ≥ 1.33.

Cpk = min( (USL − µ) / 3σ , (µ − LSL) / 3σ )

Cpk ≥ 1.33 → capable process (target)
Cpk = 1.00 → 0.27% defect rate (2700 PPM)
Cpk < 1.00 → process not capable — too many out-of-spec units

Applied to C2 ESR Across a Board Population

Three scenarios model the same ESR spec (≤ 0.10Ω) with different component lot quality. A tight MLCC lot (Cpk 1.83) has near-zero defects. A mixed or aged lot (Cpk 0.28) delivers over 20% failing units — which is exactly why lot traceability and incoming inspection matter in a manufacturing environment.

That's not a gut feel, it's a number from the statistics. I can calculate it from a sample of measurements and tell you whether a lot is safe to use before it goes into production."

3 — AC Phasor Analysis

Trigonometry · AC Circuit Analysis

The Concept

Every AC circuit is a geometry problem in disguise. Resistance R and capacitive reactance Xc form two sides of a right triangle — the impedance |Z| is the hypotenuse (Pythagorean theorem), and the phase angle φ is found with arctan. Voltage and current waveforms are then expressed as sin and cos of that angle.

Xc = 1 / (2π·f·C) ← reactance (frequency-dependent resistance)
|Z| = √(R² + Xc²) ← Pythagorean theorem
φ = arctan(Xc / R) ← phase angle (trig)
V_R = Vs · cos(φ) ← resistor voltage
V_C = Vs · sin(φ) ← capacitor voltage (lags 90°)

Check: V_R² + V_C² = Vs² ← Pythagorean identity confirms the math

Applied to the 1kHz Rail Ripple (from FFT Analysis)

Using the same 1kHz ripple frequency identified by the FFT in analysis_eet.html Plot 3 — this shows the phasor relationship between the rail impedance components and how the phase angle is measured on a two-channel oscilloscope as the time offset between V and I waveforms.

Key Talking Point
"The phase angle between voltage and current on a power rail tells you something about the reactive load on that net. I measure it on the oscilloscope by putting voltage on one channel and current on the other and reading the time offset between their zero crossings — then φ equals arctan of that time offset divided by the period times 2π. That's applied trig. And once I have the phase angle I can calculate the power factor, which tells me how efficiently the rail is delivering real power versus reactive power."

4 — PCB Trace Geometry

Plane Geometry · Distance Formula · 45° Routing

The Concept

PCB traces route at 0° and 45° angles between component pads. The exact length of each segment comes from the distance formula — plane geometry. That length then drives two engineering parameters: trace inductance (1nH per mm rule) and signal propagation time (trace length divided by the speed of light in FR4).

Segment length: L = √(Δx² + Δy²) ← distance formula
45° segment: L_45 = |Δx| · √2 ← when Δx = Δy
Trace inductance: L_nH ≈ L_mm × 1 nH/mm ← 1nH/mm rule
Travel time: t = L / v_prop ← v_prop = 0.6c in FR4

Total length = Σ all segment lengths ← sum of geometry

Applied to ADDR_BUS[4] — The Boundary-Scan Fault Net

This is the same net flagged as OPEN in the daily workflow boundary-scan fault report (U4.PIN22 → U6.PIN3). The geometry calculation gives us the trace inductance that feeds directly into the RLC Bode plot in analysis_eet.html Plot 2 — closing the loop between physical layout and electrical behavior.

"Geometry is in the job every time you touch Altium. Trace length from the distance formula directly gives you inductance — 1 nanohenry per millimeter is the rule of thumb for microstrip — and that inductance is what shifts the resonant frequency of the decoupling network. A trace that's 5mm longer than it needed to be has 5 extra nanohenries of inductance, which drops the impedance minimum by a calculable amount. That's the connection between routing geometry and signal performance."

5 — ICT Retest Modeling (Binomial Distribution)

Probability · In-Circuit Test · Intermittent Solder Joint

The Concept

An intermittent solder joint makes electrical contact only part of the time. If a joint passes contact 50% of the time, what is the probability it gets caught in n retest cycles? This is a binomial problem — each retest cycle is an independent trial with probability p of detecting the fault.

The critical insight: there is a non-trivial probability the bad joint passes every retest and ships. The math tells you when to stop retesting and escalate to engineering.

Setup

  • n = 10 — number of ICT retest cycles
  • p = 0.5 — probability joint makes contact (detected as fault) on any single cycle
  • k = 4 — number of cycles where fault was detected (joint failed open)
  • Question: What is P(k = 4 detections in 10 retests)? And what is P(joint passes ALL 10 retests)?
BINOMIAL PMF — P(X = k) = C(n,k) · p^k · (1-p)^(n-k)
P(k=4) = C(10,4) · (0.5)^4 · (0.5)^6
       = 210 · 0.0625 · 0.015625
       = 0.2051 → 20.51% chance of exactly 4 detections

P(k=0) = C(10,0) · (0.5)^0 · (0.5)^10
       = 1 · 1 · 0.000977
       = 0.0010 → 0.10% chance joint passes ALL 10 retests and ships

What This Means on the Bench

  • If a board passes ICT 6 out of 10 retests and fails 4 — the joint is intermittent, not random noise
  • P(k=0) = 0.10% means roughly 1 in 1000 of these boards would pass all retests and ship with a bad joint
  • At production volume (500 boards/week), that is 0–1 field failures per week from this joint alone
  • Decision rule: If a board fails ICT more than twice in retest, stop retesting. Escalate to engineering — the math shows retesting cannot reliably catch this fault class
Key Talking Point

"I modeled an intermittent cold solder joint at ICT using a binomial distribution — p=0.5 contact probability, n=10 retests. The math shows there's a 0.10% chance the board passes every retest and ships anyway. At production volume that's real field risk. The statistical case for escalating instead of retesting is exactly what probability is for in a test engineering role."

CAD Notes

Altium Designer · OrCAD · Eagle · IPC-2221

Altium — Trace Length Measurement

  • Select a trace → Inspect → Measure gives exact segment lengths
  • PCB Inspector → Net length field for the complete routed length
  • Use measured length as input to the inductance and travel-time calculations above
  • Verify 45° segments: Δx must equal Δy — Altium enforces this in Interactive Router

IPC-2221 — Width + Length Together

  • IPC-2221 width formula sets the minimum width for current capacity
  • Geometry model sets the maximum length for acceptable inductance
  • Both constraints must be satisfied simultaneously on power nets
  • Document both calculations in the design review — width from IPC-2221, length from √(Δx²+Δy²)

Cpk in a Manufacturing Context

  • Cpk results feed directly into incoming inspection decisions
  • Apriso / Camstar MES systems often capture component lot data — tie Cpk to lot number
  • NCR process: if a board fails ESR, pull the component lot Cpk — if Cpk < 1.33, the lot is suspect, not just that one unit
  • OrCAD BOM + lot traceability = complete statistical inference chain

Phase Angle — Scope Measurement

  • Two-channel scope: Ch1 = voltage, Ch2 = current (via current probe or sense resistor)
  • Measure time offset Δt between zero crossings → φ = (Δt / T) × 360°
  • This is arctan math made visible on the oscilloscope display
  • Power factor = cos(φ) — relevant for any AC power measurement on the board

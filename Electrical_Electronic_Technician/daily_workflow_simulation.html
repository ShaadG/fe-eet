8:00 AM – 8:30 AM Shift Handoff & Failure Log Review MES · Failure Tracking · Priorities
▶

Scenario: You arrive at the bench. The night-shift tech left three flagged units on the rework shelf and a note in the MES (Manufacturing Execution System). Your first job is not to touch the boards — it's to understand the context before you pick up a probe.

What You Actually Do

MES System (Apriso / Camstar) Failure Log
1
Log in to the MES terminal. Open the failure log for the current production order. Note which serial numbers are flagged and what symptom was recorded by the night shift (e.g., "Unit S/N 003 — no 3.3V rail at power-on").
2
Check equipment status — confirm the oscilloscope, multimeter, and boundary-scan station are powered up and showing no self-test errors before your first measurement.
3
Pull the traveler (production work order document) for each failed unit. The traveler shows which test step failed, the expected result, and the actual result that triggered the failure.
4
Prioritize: boards stuck in test block production are highest priority. Boards already on the rework shelf are second. Never change the priority order without supervisor confirmation.

8:30 AM – 10:00 AM Visual Inspection & BOM Verification IPC-A-610 · BOM · ESD · Microscope
▶

Scenario: You pick up S/N 003 — the board with a low 3.3V rail at TP3. Before connecting any power, perform a full visual inspection under the microscope and verify the BOM. Many failures are visible without a single measurement.

ESD first: Wrist strap on, connected to the bench ground strap, continuity verified before the board leaves the ESD bag.

Visual Inspection Sequence (Power Off)

BOM Rev C IPC-A-610H
1
Burnt or discolored components — char marks, brown/black discoloration on component bodies or PCB substrate. Any burn = immediate STOP, escalate before powering up.
2
Cracked solder joints — under 10× magnification look for solder joints with a dull, grayish, cracked surface. Bright, shiny, concave meniscus = good joint. Dull, convex, or fractured = cold/cracked joint.
3
Solder bridges — check fine-pitch IC pads (0.5mm pitch QFP/QFN) for solder that bridges two adjacent pads. Use the BOM to identify high-density ICs and zoom in specifically on those.
4
Tombstoned passives — 0402/0603 resistors or capacitors standing on one end. Caused by unequal reflow on both pads. Visible at 5× magnification.
5
BOM cross-check — compare physical component markings against the BOM for the suspect area (power rail ICs, regulator, decoupling caps near TP3). Confirm the correct value cap is populated, not a similar package with a different value.

IPC-A-610H — What You're Grading Against
  • Class 3 (Defense/Aerospace): Most stringent. Solder joint must show target or acceptable condition — no process indicators left uncorrected.
  • Solder joint acceptability: Concave fillet, wetting visible, minimum 75% solder fill on through-hole (Class 3 = 100% for critical joints).
  • Process indicator vs. Defect: A process indicator is a marginal condition — still functional but must be flagged. A defect is a mandatory NCR and rework. You are grading both under the microscope.
"I always do a visual inspection before applying power. IPC-A-610 gives me the vocabulary to describe what I see — whether a joint is Target, Acceptable, a Process Indicator, or a Defect. If I see a burn mark or a solder bridge, I stop and escalate before powering up, because applying voltage to a shorted board can damage additional components and make the fault harder to isolate."

Multimeter Mode Reference

1
DC Voltage (V⎓): Verify all power rails ±5% of nominal. Black probe to GND first, always. Multimeter shows DC average only — cannot detect transient sags.
2
Continuity (⟾): Power off before testing. Beep = connected. Silence = open circuit. Use to verify GND plane connections, fuse continuity, jumper placement.
3
Resistance (Ω): Power off AND discharge caps before measuring. Parallel circuit paths contaminate the reading — isolate the component or remove it from the board for a clean measurement.
4
Diode test (→|): Forward bias: 0.5–0.7V = good silicon. 0.2–0.4V = Schottky. OL reverse = good. 0V either direction = shorted junction.
"The multimeter tells me the DC steady-state condition. A rail reading 3.18V instead of 3.3V is suspicious, but the meter alone can't tell me if it's a static drop or a transient sag. That distinction matters because the fix is different — a static drop suggests a resistive fault or weak regulator; a transient sag suggests a decoupling failure. That's when I bring in the oscilloscope."

S/N 003 — Oscilloscope on TP3 (3.3V Rail)

Oscilloscope — DC Coupling AC Coupling — Noise Isolation
1
Set DC coupling, 500mV/div, 200µs/div. Connect probe to TP3, ground clip to nearby GND test point (not the chassis — board GND only). Observe the baseline.
2
Observe: the rail shows periodic sags — voltage drops to ~2.88V every ~1ms, duration ~30µs. The MCU brownout threshold is 2.97V. Each sag triggers a brownout reset → explains the MCU boot failure on S/N 005.
3
Switch to AC coupling, reduce to 100mV/div to isolate the ripple component. Measure ripple amplitude: ~420mV p-p at ~1kHz. This is the switching regulator frequency coupling through a degraded C2.
CH1: TP3 — 3.3V Rail DC coupling 500mV/div 200µs/div Trigger: falling edge, 3.0V
Waveform interpretation: Periodic voltage sags visible at ~1ms intervals, dropping to 2.88V for ~30µs. This is a classic high-ESR bypass capacitor signature — C2 cannot supply the instantaneous current demanded by the MCU during active cycles. Root cause confirmed: replace C2 with a low-ESR MLCC (ESR < 0.05Ω).

S/N 003 — C2 Replacement Procedure

J-STD-001 (Soldering) IPC-7711/7721 (Rework)
1
Verify NCR is written first. Never rework a board without a documented NCR. The NCR contains: board S/N, defect description, approved repair action, technician name, and date. This is the traceability record.
2
Pre-heat the board to 100°C on the rework station hotplate to reduce thermal shock. C2 is a 0805 MLCC — thermal gradient cracking is a real failure mode if you apply soldering iron heat without pre-warming.
3
Use hot-air rework station (350°C, low airflow) to remove C2. Collect the removed component — it goes to the FA (failure analysis) sample bag, not the trash, in case engineering needs to confirm the ESR measurement.
4
Clean the pads with flux remover and inspect under 10× magnification — pads must be flat, shiny, free of residue before placing the new component.
5
Place new C2 (verified against BOM part number, correct value, correct package). Apply minimal flux, reflow with soldering iron using J-STD-001 technique: iron tip to pad, not to component body. Solder flows in 2–3 seconds. Remove iron, allow to cool undisturbed.

Post-Rework IPC-A-610H Inspection Criteria

IPC-A-610H — SMT Chip Component (C2, 0805 Capacitor) — Class 3
  • Solder fillet: Must be present on both ends. Minimum fillet height = 25% of component height. Concave meniscus preferred. Convex = process indicator (Class 3 = must correct).
  • End joint width: Minimum 75% of component width. Less than 50% = defect.
  • Wetting: Solder must show wetting on both the component metallization and the PCB pad — bright, smooth surface contact. Dull or peeling = cold joint = defect.
  • Overhang: Component must not extend beyond the pad edge by more than 25% of component width (Class 3).
  • No flux residue: Class 3 requires cleaning of all ionic flux residues. Test with white residue test or ionic contamination tester if required by the process spec.
After inspection: If the joint passes IPC-A-610H Class 3 criteria, sign off on the NCR and return the board to test. If it fails, rework again — document each attempt. After 3 rework cycles on the same location, the board must be escalated to the Test Engineer for a disposition decision (scrap, use-as-is with engineering sign-off, or additional rework).
"I only desolder a component after I've confirmed it's the root cause through measurements — not based on a guess. And I write the NCR before touching the iron, not after. That sequence matters for traceability: if the board fails again after rework, engineering needs to know exactly what was done, by whom, and when. IPC-A-610 gives me the objective pass/fail criteria for the solder joint inspection — it's not subjective. Either the fillet meets the minimum requirements for Class 3, or it doesn't."

Calibration Check — End of Shift

1
Multimeter: Touch probes together in Ω mode — reading should be 0.1–0.5Ω maximum (lead resistance). Verify battery indicator. Check cal sticker — if overdue, tag out of service and notify metrology.
2
Oscilloscope: Connect CH1 probe to the scope's own calibration output (typically 1kHz, 1Vpp square wave). Run probe compensation — adjust trimmer screw until corners of square wave are flat (no overshoot, no rounding). A miscompensated probe produces measurement errors up to 20%.
3
Soldering station: Verify tip temperature with thermocouple probe. Log actual vs. setpoint temperature. If >±15°C deviation, replace tip and re-verify before using on production boards.
Why calibration matters at shift end: The next shift will rely on the equipment you used today. If your scope's probe was miscompensated all afternoon, every waveform measurement you logged is suspect. Catching it at the end of your shift and correcting it — and noting it in the equipment log — is the professional standard.

Escalation Decision — S/N 005

S/N 005 (MCU no boot, UART silent) was not resolved today. The correct action:

  • Write a complete summary of everything you tried and observed — no assumptions, no guesses. The engineer needs raw data: what did you measure, at which test point, at which time, and what was the result.
  • Do not guess at root cause in the escalation note — stick to facts. "TP3 = 3.18V, oscilloscope shows 2.88V sags, MCU does not respond on UART after 10 power cycles" is better than "probably a bad MCU."
  • Tag the board clearly — "Hold for EE — NCR-2024-0847-005 — Do not test."
Why this matters in an interview: Interviewers ask "what do you do when you're stuck?" The correct answer is: document everything, escalate with data (not opinions), and tag the board with a clear hold status. The wrong answer is: keep probing hoping something changes, or guess and replace components without authorization.
"At the end of the day I close out every NCR I opened. The NCR isn't just paperwork — it's the record that connects a board's symptom to the root cause and the repair action. If that board fails in the field six months later, the NCR is what engineering uses to determine whether the repair was the issue or something else. I also do a quick calibration check on the test equipment I used, because if a tool is out of cal, every measurement I took with it today is questionable."

Question 1 of 4 7A — Frequency / Transient Response

A series RC circuit is connected to a 10 V DC step source at t = 0. The capacitor is initially uncharged. At what voltage will the capacitor be charged to at t = 2 ms?

R = 2 kΩ = 2,000 Ω C = 0.5 µF = 0.5 × 10⁻⁶ F V_source = 10 V (step at t = 0) v_C(0) = 0 V

Solution — 7A: RC Transient Response

Formula (FE Handbook p. 363 — RC and RL Transients):

v_C(t) = v_C(0)·e^(−t/RC) + V·(1 − e^(−t/RC))

With v_C(0) = 0 and τ = RC:

v_C(t) = V · (1 − e^(−t/τ))

Step 1 — Find τ:

τ = RC = 2,000 × 0.5×10⁻⁶ = 1×10⁻³ s = 1 ms

Step 2 — Evaluate at t = 2 ms = 2τ:

v_C(2ms) = 10 · (1 − e^(−2ms/1ms)) = 10 · (1 − e^(−2)) = 10 · (1 − 0.1353) = 10 × 0.8647 = 8.647 V

Answer: (C) 8.65 V

Note: (B) 6.32 V = V·(1−e⁻¹) is the voltage at t = 1τ, a common distractor.

FE Handbook ref: "RC and RL Transients" — v_C(t) = v_C(0)e^(−t/RC) + V(1−e^(−t/RC))
Question 2 of 4 7B — Resonance

For the series RLC circuit below, what is the quality factor Q at resonance?

R = 5 Ω L = 50 mH = 0.05 H C = 20 µF = 20 × 10⁻⁶ F

Solution — 7B: Series Resonance

Formula (FE Handbook p. 363 — Resonance):

ω₀ = 1 / √(LC) rad/s (resonant frequency) Q = ω₀L / R (series resonance Q) BW = ω₀ / Q rad/s (bandwidth)

Step 1 — Find ω₀:

ω₀ = 1 / √(0.05 × 20×10⁻⁶) = 1 / √(1×10⁻⁶) = 1 / 10⁻³ = 1,000 rad/s

Step 2 — Find Q:

Q = ω₀L / R = (1,000 × 0.05) / 5 = 50 / 5 = 10

Step 3 — Verify using alternate form:

Q = 1 / (ω₀CR) = 1 / (1000 × 20×10⁻⁶ × 5) = 1 / 0.1 = 10 ✓

Answer: (D) Q = 10

Bandwidth = ω₀/Q = 1000/10 = 100 rad/s. Higher Q → narrower bandwidth → more selective filter.

FE Handbook ref: Series Resonance — Q = ω₀L/R = 1/(ω₀CR), BW = ω₀/Q
Question 3 of 4 7C — Laplace Transforms

What is the one-sided (unilateral) Laplace transform F(s) of the signal f(t) = 3 sin(2t) u(t), where u(t) is the unit step function?

f(t) = 3 sin(2t) · u(t) Recall: ℒ{sin(ωt) u(t)} = ω / (s² + ω²)

Solution — 7C: Laplace Transform

Standard pair (from Laplace transform table):

ℒ{sin(ωt) u(t)} = ω / (s² + ω²)

Step 1 — Identify ω:

f(t) = 3 sin(2t) → amplitude = 3, ω = 2

Step 2 — Apply linearity:

F(s) = 3 · ℒ{sin(2t)} = 3 · [2 / (s² + 2²)] = 3 · [2 / (s² + 4)] = 6 / (s² + 4)

Answer: (B) F(s) = 6 / (s² + 4)

(A) is wrong because ω = 2 was not placed in the numerator.
(C) has the wrong denominator constant (should be ω² = 4, not 2).
(D) is the cosine transform — ℒ{cos(ωt)} = s/(s²+ω²).

Laplace table: ℒ{sin(ωt)} = ω/(s²+ω²), ℒ{cos(ωt)} = s/(s²+ω²)
Question 4 of 4 7D — Transfer Functions

A second-order LTI system has the transfer function shown below. What are the poles of H(s)?

H(s) = 50 / (s² + 7s + 50)

Solution — 7D: Transfer Function Poles

Method: Set denominator = 0 and apply quadratic formula.

Denominator: s² + 7s + 50 = 0 Quadratic formula: s = [−b ± √(b²−4ac)] / 2a a=1, b=7, c=50

Step 1 — Discriminant:

Δ = b² − 4ac = 49 − 4(1)(50) = 49 − 200 = −151

Step 2 — Poles:

s = (−7 ± √(−151)) / 2 = (−7 ± j√151) / 2 = −3.5 ± j · (12.288/2) = −3.5 ± j · 6.144

Answer: (B) s = −3.5 ± j6.14

Damping check: Standard 2nd-order form: s² + 2ζωₙs + ωₙ²
ωₙ = √50 ≈ 7.07 rad/s, 2ζωₙ = 7 → ζ = 7/(2×7.07) ≈ 0.495
Since 0 < ζ < 1, the system is underdamped. Poles are complex conjugates in the left-half plane (Re < 0), so the system is stable.

(D) is wrong — it uses ωₙ = √50 ≈ 7.07 for the imaginary part instead of the actual imaginary component √(ωₙ² − σ²) = √(50 − 12.25) = √37.75 ≈ 6.14.

2nd-order form: s² + 2ζωₙs + ωₙ² → poles at s = −ζωₙ ± jωₙ√(1−ζ²)
